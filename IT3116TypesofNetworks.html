Introduction to Computer Networks

Computer networks are systems that allow two or more devices to connect and share data or resources. They are broadly categorized based on their geographical span, ranging from small personal networks to global interconnected systems. The choice of network type depends on the required coverage, number of users, data transfer rate, and cost.

Types of Computer Networks

1. Personal Area Network (PAN) üë®‚Äçüíª

A PAN is the smallest and most basic type of network, designed for individual use within a very limited personal space.

Key Characteristics:

  • Range: Typically covers a few meters, up to 10 meters (33 feet).
  • Connectivity: Uses technologies like Bluetooth, infrared, or USB.
  • Devices: Connects personal devices such as smartphones, tablets, laptops, wireless headsets, and printers.

Example: Connecting your wireless headphones to your phone, or using a Bluetooth mouse with your laptop.

2. Local Area Network (LAN) üè†

A LAN connects devices within a limited geographical area, such as a home, office building, or school campus.

Key Characteristics:

  • Range: Confined to a single building or a small cluster of buildings.
  • Connectivity: Uses Ethernet (wired) or Wi-Fi (wireless).
  • Devices: Connects computers, servers, printers, and other network-enabled devices.
  • Data Transfer Rate: High, typically from 10 Mbps to 10 Gbps.

Example: All computers and printers connected within a single office building.

3. Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN) üì∂

A WLAN is a type of LAN that uses wireless communication to connect devices, eliminating the need for physical cables.

Key Characteristics:

  • Range: Similar to LANs, typically within a building or campus.
  • Connectivity: Uses Wi-Fi (IEEE 802.11 standards).
  • Flexibility: Offers mobility to users within the coverage area.

Example: Connecting your laptop to the internet via your home Wi-Fi router.

4. Metropolitan Area Network (MAN) üèôÔ∏è

A MAN covers a larger geographical area than a LAN but is smaller than a WAN, typically spanning an entire city or a large campus.

Key Characteristics:

  • Range: Covers a metropolitan area, connecting multiple LANs.
  • Connectivity: Often uses fiber optic cables or microwave links.
  • Ownership: Usually owned by a single entity (e.g., a city council) or a consortium.

Example: A network connecting different university campuses within a city, or a cable TV network.

5. Wide Area Network (WAN) üåç

A WAN spans large geographical areas, connecting cities, countries, or even continents. The internet itself is the largest WAN.

Key Characteristics:

  • Range: Connects geographically dispersed LANs and MANs.
  • Connectivity: Uses technologies like fiber optic lines, satellite links, and leased lines.
  • Data Transfer Rate: Varies widely, often lower than LANs due to long distances, but constantly improving.

Example: The network connecting all branches of a multinational corporation, or the global internet.

6. Global Area Network (GAN) üõ∞Ô∏è

A GAN is a network used to support mobile communication across an arbitrary number of wireless LANs, satellite coverage areas, etc. The most prominent example of a GAN is the internet.

Key Characteristics:

  • Range: Connects mobile users across the globe.
  • Connectivity: Utilizes a combination of wired, wireless, and satellite technologies.
  • Focus: Primarily supports global mobility and communication.

Example: Multinational organizations with mobile employees accessing their corporate network from anywhere in the world.

7. Storage Area Network (SAN) üóÑÔ∏è

A SAN is a dedicated high-speed network that provides access to consolidated, block-level data storage.

Key Characteristics:

  • Purpose: Provides shared pools of storage devices to multiple servers.
  • Connectivity: Uses technologies like Fibre Channel or iSCSI.
  • Performance: Optimized for high-speed data transfer between servers and storage.

Example: Used in data centers and large enterprises for efficient data management and backups.

8. Enterprise Private Network (EPN) üè¢

An EPN is a computer network built by an enterprise to interconnect its various company sites (e.g., production sites, offices, shops) in a way that all shared resources can be accessed by authorized users.

Key Characteristics:

  • Ownership: Owned and managed by a single organization.
  • Purpose: Connects all company locations securely.
  • Security: High-level security measures are typically implemented.

Example: A large corporation with multiple offices using a private network to share resources and communicate internally.

9. Virtual Private Network (VPN) üîí

A VPN extends a private network across a public network (like the internet), enabling users to send and receive data as if their computing devices were directly connected to the private network.

Key Characteristics:

  • Security: Creates a secure, encrypted connection over an insecure network.
  • Accessibility: Allows remote users to securely access a private network.
  • Cost-Effective: Leverages existing internet infrastructure.

Example: An employee working from home securely accessing their company's internal network resources.
